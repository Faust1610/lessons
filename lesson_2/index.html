General information about the brand

Ferrari S.p.A. is an Italian company producing sports and racing cars, based in Maranello. Founded in 1947 by Enzo Ferrari as Scuderia Ferrari, the company sponsored racing drivers and produced racing cars until 1947. Since 1947, it began producing sports cars approved for use on public roads under the Ferrari S.p.A. brand.Throughout its history, the company has been involved in various racing events, especially Formula 1, where it has had the greatest success. The Ferrari emblem is a prancing stallion on a yellow background. The traditional color of cars is red, but the company produces cars in other colors.

Emblem

The Ferrari emblem - a prancing stallion on a yellow background - first appeared on printed materials and official company documents in 1929. However, at that time, the Prancing Stallion was not depicted on the cars, as they belonged to Alfa Romeo and had their own logo in the form of a cloverleaf on a white triangular background. The image of a prancing horse was not unique then - for example, since the 1860s it was on steam equipment (locomotives and rollers) of the British company Aveling and
Enzo Ferrari wrote in his memoirs:
“As a factory mark, I continued to use the image of a rearing horse, which I first appeared on Scuderia Ferrari cars. The story of this rearing horse is simple and funny. Francesco Baracca carried this emblem on his fighter plane from the First World War. He was shot down near Montello. When I competed at the Circuito del Savio in Ravenna in 1923, I met Count Enrico Baracca, the father of the hero. He introduced me to his wife, Countess Paolina Baracca, who one day suggested: Ferrari, why don’t you depict my son’s rearing horse on your racing car? He will bring you good luck.I still keep a photograph of the ace with a dedication from his parents, in which they entrust me with this sign with a horse. The horse was and remains black. I added a golden background, which is the color of the city of Modena.”
The Scuderia Ferrari emblem looks like a triangular shield, and the Ferrari plant logo looks like a rectangle, with a striped ribbon of the Italian flag.

Activities

The company produces racing and sports cars. In 2008, the company produced 6,662 cars (1.2% more than in 2007). The number of personnel of the company at the end of 2008 was 3,017 people (3.1% more than in 2007). Turnover in 2008 amounted to 1.921 billion euros (15.2% more than in 2007).
The racing division of Scuderia Ferrari (Italian: Scuderia Ferrari) competes in Formula 1 racing and is the most successful in the history of racing (they became world champions 15 times and won the Constructors' Championship 16 times).

Vehicle history

1930—1950

The company (its original name was Auto Avio Costruzioni) was founded in 1929 by racer, designer and tester of Alfa Romeo cars Enzo Ferrari. Initially, it produced various equipment for cars. The cars made by the company were produced under the Alfa Romeo brand. Ferrari had an agreement with this company. The first car bearing the actual name “Ferrari” appeared in 1946. It was the Ferrari 125 model, with a powerful 12-cylinder aluminum engine, designed to realize the dream of its creator: to give an ordinary road car racing properties without sacrificing comfort. Enzo Ferrari chose a prancing stallion on a yellow background as the company's trademark.
By the end of 1947, there were already two modifications of the Ferrari engine, and its displacement increased in the 166 model from 1496 to 1995 cm³. The following year, Ferrari's own team won the Mille Miglia and Targa Florio races for the first time. 1949 brought the team another triumph in the same competition, and a little later a victory in the 24 Hours of Le Mans.

1950—1960

In 1951, the milestone model 340 America appeared with an engine originally developed for the Ferrari GT with a displacement of 4.1 liters. In 1953, the same car was equipped with a 4.5-liter engine and received a new name - 375 America. In the same year, Ferrari introduced the 250 Europa, with a three-liter engine.
In total, by the beginning of 1954, Enzo Ferrari had produced about 200 of his cars in an exclusive road version and 250 racing models. When creating his road cars, Ferrari turned to different design companies, making their models different from each other. But the 1954 250 GT marked the beginning of a long-term and fruitful collaboration with Pininfarina, which perfectly matched its bodies to the new short-wheelbase chassis, in which the drive rear axle was suspended on springs.
1958 Ferrari 250 GT California Spyder
The 250 GT model was already produced not only as a coupe, but also as a convertible, and the custom-made 250 GT California, which appeared in 1959, is a striking example of the open sports-type 250 GT model. In 1958, Pininfarina created a body for the 250 GT model with a specifically angular shape: these cars, equipped with a 12-cylinder engine, externally gave the impression of enormous power, ideally obedient to any desires of their owners.
The 375 America and the 410 Super America that replaced it in 1956 (only 14 of them were produced) were intended for the “powers that be”: their design evoked a feeling of strength and self-confidence.
From 1957 to 1962, a modified Ferrari 250 GT California was produced with a predatory pointed profile and a removable roof. This car scored its first victory in the 1960 Tourist Trophy race at Goodwood Circuit.

1960—1970

Ferrari owns the most memorable cars of the 60s of the XX century: the 250 GT in 1960 was transformed into the 250 GTE fastback with a “2+2” body, elegant and popular, on the basis of which the 330 GT “2+2” was created in 1964 four-liter engine and original “squinting” headlights. From 1962 to 1964, one of the most significant cars in history, the 250 GTO (“Gran Turismo Omologata”), was built on the basis of the 250 GT. A total of 39 cars were produced with a 3-liter V12 engine producing 300 hp. s., having a 5-speed gearbox. The weight of the car was 1100 kg, the wheelbase was 2400 mm, and the maximum speed was more than 283 km/h.
Subsequently, in 2013, a 1963 Ferrari 250 GTO was sold for a record $52 million, making it the most expensive car in the world.
The feminine, graceful, swift Berlinetta Lusso of 1962, reaching speeds of over 225 km/h, remains unsurpassed. The 330 GT “2+2” was replaced in 1967 by the 365 GT “2+2” with self-balancing rear suspension and power steering. In 1971 it was replaced by the 365 GT4, which was more austere in keeping with the spirit of the times.
In 1966, the company developed a new V-engine, derived from racing engines: a 12-cylinder, four-cam, with two camshafts in each cylinder head, a dry sump lubrication system, providing high torque and flexibility, with a power of 300 hp. With.
Ferrari DaytonaIn 1968, Ferrari released the legendary Daytona, or 365 GTB/4, with a front-mounted 4.4-liter V-12 engine producing 352 horsepower. with., developing super speed - 282 km/h. This model is outwardly discreet and functional.

1970—1990

In the early 1970s, the “Dino” model appeared, named after Commendatore’s deceased son, with a centrally located FIAT V-6-cylinder engine; The model was also produced in an 8-cylinder version, but the 6-cylinder model is considered a classic. At one time, Dino was actually a separate brand. The Dino-206 GT car has been produced since 1967 with a V6 engine (1987 cm3 180 hp at 8000 rpm; since 1969 - “246 GT” - 2418 cm3, 195 hp at 7600 rpm ). Exactly the same engines, unconventional for Ferrari, were installed on the sports Fiat Dino.
The 365 GT4, or Berlinetta Boxer, produced in 1971, had many features of Ferrari racing cars: its 4.4-liter engine could reach a speed of 275 km/h; it was located horizontally in the middle of the body, and the gearbox for more efficient use The internal volume of the body was located under the engine. The chassis also used the experience of racing cars: a tubular space frame and steel body panels. Based on the Ferrari 308 GT4, elegant models were made with Pininfarina bodies; equipped with V-shaped 8-cylinder engines, they attracted the attention of buyers until early 80s.
Ferrari TestarossaBy installing the horizontal 12-cylinder Boxer racing engine 512 BB in the Testarossa, the company created a massive, heavy machine that meets the most demanding requirements of both sports racers and celebrity clients. The bright, eye-catching design of Pininfarin carried the quintessence of the advertising spirit, and its creator sought not only an external effect: ribbed side air intakes, which found many imitators, served to supply air to the side radiators, replacing the front radiator 512 BB. The technical performance of the model was also impeccable: the five-liter Testarossa engine, with four valves per cylinder, reached a power of 390 hp. s., and its maximum speed is 274 km/h.
Ferrari F40In 1987, the founder of the company, Enzo Ferrari, headed the team of designers who prepared the F40 model, designated as “the sum of the company’s efforts over the years of its existence.” The F40 dates back to the 1984 GTO and, at first glance, has much in common with the Ferrari 308 GTB, but has significant technical innovations: turbocharging, a V-shaped 8-cylinder longitudinal (rather than transverse) engine mounted on a tubular frame, reinforced with Kevlar load-bearing panels, super power 478 hp. With. - everything suggests that only the configuration remains the same. The body of this supermodel is made of carbon fiber and Kevlar, and its cramped interior fully corresponds to the sporty essence of the car: it does not even have a mechanism for adjusting the seats. Amazing technical performance and no comfort - that’s the motto of the 1118-kilogram Ferrari F40: the rigid suspension did not absorb road irregularities, the steering responded to every pothole, and the magnificent engine demonstrated endless power. Demanding, uncontrollably energetic and attractive - this was the latest brainchild of the company’s founder.
Ferrari cars, controlled by FIAT since 1969 and finally transferred to the corporation in 1989, have a reputation as the most expensive in the world. Each of them is a legend.

1990—2000

In the fall of 1992, a high-power rear-wheel drive sports coupe with a classic layout, the 456 GT/GTA, was released in Paris. Design: Pininfarina. GTA - March 1996 (Geneva). ASR, modernized body, suspension and interior (4 seats) - March 1998, Geneva.
In May 1994, the F355 was released, a sports car successor to the 348 GTB/GTS. The cheapest and most popular model of the company. Design: Pininfarina. In the spring of 1995, the Spider appeared - a convertible. Berlinetta is a coupe, GTS is a coupe with a removable hardtop, F1 is a racing car-style transmission control. Ceased production of Berlinetta/Targa - summer 1997.
The 550 Maranello successor to the Testarossa/512 is based on the 456 GT. Design: Pininfarina. Debut - July 20, 1996. The 360 Modena is the successor to the F355 Berlinetta. Design: Pininfarina. The premiere took place in March 1999 in Geneva. Start of sales - summer 1999.
